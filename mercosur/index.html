Section 1/5

A Puzzle: Why Does Mercosur Stay Together?

Since 1995, Mercosur has kept a common external tariff and an internal free trade area. Uruguay threatens exit, external negotiations stall with partners such as China, United States and European Union, yet the bloc persists and no member has actually left.

In the job market paper, this is framed as a stability puzzle: exit threats are persistent, but the customs union neither breaks nor delivers many large external agreements.

What we see in the data

  • → Uruguay explores bilateral ideas with partners such as United States and China
  • → Negotiations with large partners drag on for many years
  • → The bloc keeps a common tariff and internal free trade in place

See the introduction and the four historical episodes discussed in the paper for details on these tensions.

Why this matters

  • → It affects supply chains for nearly 280 million people in South America
  • → It speaks to the broader question of whether regional agreements help or hinder global liberalization
  • → It links external bargaining, internal enforcement and sector specific shocks
Key insight: Small members gain bargaining power when they negotiate as part of the bloc, while large members face concentrated losses in manufacturing sectors. This combination helps explain why Uruguay keeps a strong interest in staying and why Brazil and Argentina often slow down external deals.

This summary mirrors the abstract and results sections of the job market paper.

How Mercosur Works

Who Trades with Whom?

Over two decades, China becomes the main external partner for every member in both exports and imports, while United States and European Union remain important but secondary. This happens even with modest tariff changes, which means quantity responses are doing most of the work.

Brazil: diversified manufacturing with strong trade to China and United States
Argentina: mix of agriculture and industry, also reorienting exports toward China
Uruguay: small, high value agriculture, with balanced links to China, European Union and United States
Paraguay: heavier reliance on intra bloc trade and regional neighbors

Trade shares of main partners

Why Uruguay Gains from Staying

In the quantitative exercises, bloc negotiations with China raise Uruguay welfare between about 0.18 and 0.46 percentage points, while the gains from partial deviations where Uruguay negotiates alone are essentially zero on average across years.

The same pattern appears when the partner is United States: bloc deals deliver larger and more stable gains than going alone, once reciprocity and the common external tariff are taken into account.

Uruguay inside Mercosur

  • ✓ Negotiates together with Brazil and Argentina, so market size is larger
  • ✓ Receives better tariff cuts from partners when reciprocity uses the bloc as reference
  • ✓ Gains in sectors where it has comparative advantage, such as meat and oil seeds

Uruguay alone

  • ✗ Much weaker bargaining position
  • ✗ Harder to obtain deep cuts from large partners
  • ✗ Welfare gains are tiny and close to zero in most counterfactuals

Why Large Countries Block Deals

For Brazil and Argentina, bloc negotiations with China produce small aggregate welfare gains but very large employment losses in a few manufacturing sectors. That sector concentration makes it politically attractive to delay or block external agreements.

Largest sector losses under a Mercosur China deal

Apparel (Brazil)≈ −15% employment
Textiles (Brazil)≈ −5.5% employment
Leather (Brazil)≈ −3.4% employment

These numbers are based on the table of labor share changes in the Mercosur China scenario, where apparel alone loses about fifteen percent of employment in Brazil and similar though smaller losses appear in Argentina.

Losses are concentrated in a small group of politically vocal manufacturing sectors such as apparel, textiles and leather. Gains are more diffuse, spread across consumers and primary sectors such as meat, oil seeds and sugar. This asymmetry helps explain why large members often slow down external negotiations even when the bloc as a whole would obtain modest net gains.
