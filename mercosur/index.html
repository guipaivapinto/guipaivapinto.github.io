A Puzzle: Why Does Mercosur Stay Together?

Since 1995, Mercosur has kept a common external tariff and an institutional rule that members negotiate trade agreements as a bloc. At the same time, small members repeatedly threaten exit, external negotiations stall, yet the bloc persists.

The paper asks whether reciprocity based external negotiations together with sector level shocks can explain both the slow progress of new trade agreements and the surprising stability of the customs union.

What we see

  • → Uruguay publicly questions the rule that Mercosur must negotiate as a bloc
  • → Uruguay floats China and United States bilateral deals
  • → External talks with China, the United States and the European Union drag for many years
  • → The bloc never actually breaks

Why it matters

  • → Shapes supply chains for about two hundred eighty million people
  • → Stability parallels other regional agreements that look fragile on paper
  • → Sector shocks drive political pressure and change who wants external deals
Key insight: Small members gain bargaining power when they stay inside the customs union, while large members fear concentrated manufacturing losses from ambitious external deals. The same forces that make external agreements hard to sign also help the bloc remain intact.

How Mercosur Works

Customs union: internal tariffs are close to zero, common external tariff around ten percent with many product level exemptions.

Recent reforms expand the lists of exemptions to the common external tariff. Brazil plans to move from about one hundred to one hundred fifty tariff lines by 2028, Argentina mirrors this change, Uruguay from roughly two hundred twenty five to two hundred seventy five lines by 2029, and Paraguay from about six hundred forty nine to six hundred ninety nine lines by 2030. The model uses effective applied tariffs so that these exemptions are reflected in the counterfactuals.

Who Trades with Whom?

China rises to the main trading partner for all members by 2017, while the United States and the European Union remain important but secondary.

Using GTAP eleven data from 2004 to 2017, the share of China in extra bloc trade rises steadily for every member and overtakes the United States and the European Union by the end of the period. Brazil and Uruguay show the largest proportional increases, Paraguay keeps a stronger focus on intra bloc commerce, and Uruguay maintains a balanced split across the three partners.

Brazil: diversified manufacturing
Argentina: agriculture and industry mix
Uruguay: small, high value agriculture and services
Paraguay: relies more on intra bloc trade

Trade shares for extra bloc trade (qualitative summary)

Between 2004 and 2017, China share climbs for exports and imports of all members, United States share falls especially for exports, and the European Union declines slowly but stays relevant, particularly for Uruguay.

Why Uruguay Gains from Staying

The quantitative part of the paper compares reciprocal free trade agreements signed by Mercosur as a bloc with hypothetical bilateral deviations by Uruguay with China and the United States. For Uruguay, the welfare gains from signing as part of the customs union are clearly positive and reach at most about half a percent of consumption across years, while the corresponding gains from signing the same deals alone are very close to zero once reciprocity and partner responses are imposed.

These counterfactuals are used to evaluate incentives to deviate from the customs union and to revisit the puzzle of repeated exit threats. Uruguay gains from better access for meat, oilseeds, and other primary exports under bloc wide bargaining. At the same time, the largest job losses arise in light manufacturing sectors that account for a smaller share of total production, which makes staying in the customs union more attractive in the model.

Welfare gains for Uruguay in the counterfactuals

Mercosur–China deal, Uruguay inside the bloc Positive welfare gains below roughly 0.5 percent of consumption
Mercosur–United States deal, Uruguay inside the bloc Smaller but still positive welfare gains, again below about 0.5 percent
Uruguay alone with China or United States Gains close to zero once reciprocity conditions and partner reactions are enforced

Why bloc bargaining helps Uruguay

  • ✓ Larger joint market makes reciprocity constraints less binding for Uruguay
  • ✓ Better terms for meat, oilseeds, and other primary exports
  • ✓ Adverse effects fall on small manufacturing sectors, which have lower production shares

Why unilateral deviations are less attractive

  • ✓ Bilateral deals without the bloc trigger smaller partner concessions
  • ✓ Reciprocity conditions restrict tariff cuts when Uruguay negotiates alone
  • ✓ Once the EU–Mercosur agreement is taken as background, the extra gains from unilateral Uruguay deals fall further, as illustrated for the years around 2017 in the paper

Why Large Members Block Deals

In the Mercosur–China scenario highlighted in the paper, aggregate welfare gains for Brazil are small, and Uruguay gains more in the aggregate, but both countries experience very large employment losses in a few manufacturing industries. These concentrated losses help explain persistent political resistance to deep external liberalization, even when the customs union as a whole could gain.

Industry level losses in the Mercosur–China scenario

Brazil, wearing apparel (Table 10, MS–CHN 2017) ΔL ≈ −15.35%, ΔL share ≈ −0.46 percentage points
Brazil, manufactures nec (Table 10) ΔL ≈ −1.68%, ΔL share ≈ −0.10 percentage points
Uruguay, wearing apparel (Table 11, MS–CHN 2017) ΔL ≈ −33.36%, ΔL share ≈ −0.34 percentage points
Uruguay, manufactures nec (Table 11) ΔL ≈ −5.42%, ΔL share ≈ −0.49 percentage points

Tables 10 and 11 in the paper show that in the Mercosur–China negotiation, clothing and other labor intensive manufactures in Brazil and Uruguay suffer large discrete losses. In Brazil, wearing apparel loses about fifteen percent of its jobs and its employment share falls by roughly 0.46 percentage points, while manufactures nec loses around 1.68 percent of jobs and 0.10 percentage points of its labor share. In Uruguay, wearing apparel employment falls by more than one third and its share in total employment declines by about 0.34 percentage points, and manufactures nec loses more than five percent of jobs and almost half a percentage point of its employment share. These sector level numbers help explain why governments in Brazil and Uruguay face strong organized resistance from specific industries when considering ambitious tariff cuts with China, even when the customs union structure itself is stable and Uruguay gains from staying inside the bloc.

This page is a simplified interactive companion to the job market paper “External Negotiations and Customs Union Stability”.

The full paper develops a customs union model with reciprocity based bargaining, calibrates it to GTAP data for Mercosur and its main partners, and uses it to simulate counterfactual external negotiations such as Mercosur–China and Mercosur–United States, both at the bloc level and with unilateral deviations. The goal is to explain the puzzle of why Mercosur remains stable, despite repeated threats of exit, rising use of tariff exemptions, and long standing external negotiations that rarely reach implementation.
