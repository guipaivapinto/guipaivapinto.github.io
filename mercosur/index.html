Section 1/5

A puzzle: Why does Mercosur stay together?

Since 1995 Mercosur has kept a common external tariff and an internal free trade area. At the same time members routinely threaten to walk away. Uruguay openly explores bilateral deals with China and the United States while Brazil and Argentina complain about internal asymmetries and sectoral losses. External negotiations stall but the customs union does not break.

The job market paper behind this page asks a simple question: when a customs union faces big external partners like China the United States and the European Union who really gains from staying inside the bloc and who would rather negotiate alone.

What the paper does

The paper builds a quantitative trade model with external bargaining for Mercosur. It uses sector level trade data from 2004 to 2017 and simulates counterfactual negotiations with China the United States and the European Union. For each scenario it compares outcomes when:

  • Every member negotiates alone with the partner
  • Mercosur negotiates as a single bloc with a common external tariff
  • One member deviates while others keep the bloc arrangement

This gives model based measures of gains and losses in real income and sectoral profits for each country and lets us ask whether the union is internally stable when external offers arrive.

Key message: Small members like Uruguay tend to gain more in percentage of GDP when Mercosur bargains as a bloc while large members gain less and face concentrated losses in a few industries. This basic asymmetry already hints at why external talks stall without the union dissolving.

Mercosur trade policy in practice

Mercosur is more than a low tariff club. Members apply a common external tariff to most products and commit not to sign independent free trade agreements that change those external tariffs unilaterally. This makes any negotiation with large partners such as the European Union China or the United States effectively a bloc wide decision.

The paper takes that institutional reality seriously. The counterfactuals always respect reciprocity in trade policy and the no import subsidy constraint while allowing for asymmetric sectoral tariffs across partners. Internal deviations from the common external tariff are modeled explicitly so we can see how one country leaving or bending the rule affects everyone else.

External negotiations that shape the debate

  • Long running talks between the European Union and Mercosur
  • Uruguay exploring bilateral agreements with China and the United States
  • Brazil and Argentina reacting to potential losses in sensitive industries

In the model the European Union China and the United States each face the choice of bargaining with Mercosur as a whole or with individual members. The customs union constraint means that a deal with one member can upset the internal balance and trigger exit threats by others.

Partners and trade patterns

The starting point for the quantitative exercise is detailed bilateral trade data. The paper observes flows between Mercosur members and major partners from 2004 to 2017 at the sector level and reconstructs the pattern of protection across agriculture manufacturing and services.

Over this period China becomes a central buyer for South American exports while the European Union and the United States remain key destinations for higher value manufactures. Within Mercosur Brazil is the main market for neighbors industrial goods while Argentina and Uruguay specialize in different baskets of agricultural and light manufacturing products.

China

Strong growth in demand for soy meat and resource based products makes China an especially attractive partner for Uruguay and Brazil and raises the stakes of any China Mercosur deal.

United States

The United States matters both as a buyer of manufactures and as a reference point for earlier negotiations such as the aborted United States Uruguay deal in the mid two thousands that motivates several counterfactuals.

European Union

The European Union combines relatively low external tariffs in many sectors with political concerns about agriculture and deforestation. In the model this shows up as scope for mutually beneficial cuts that are still hard to implement.

Why countries like Uruguay gain from staying

The counterfactuals show that when Mercosur negotiates jointly with China or the United States the smallest member Uruguay tends to gain more in percentage of GDP than Brazil or Argentina. Those gains come both from better access to the external partner and from continued preferential access to the large neighbors markets.

Sector level results reveal especially strong increases in profits and employment in meat dairy and some manufacturing activities when Uruguay participates in bloc negotiations instead of going alone. In the joint China and United States scenarios these sectors account for most of the labor reallocation toward expanding export opportunities.

Counterfactual thought experiment

Imagine Uruguay signs an independent free trade agreement with China while the rest of Mercosur keeps the common external tariff. The model tracks how tariffs change across all partners and sectors and recomputes trade flows production and wages in every country.

Even in that favorable scenario Uruguay still values access to Brazilian and Argentine markets. The paper shows that joint bargaining often delivers larger aggregate gains than the most optimistic unilateral deviation once the full general equilibrium response is taken into account.

Link back to the political debate

This helps interpret repeated exit threats by Uruguay. The country can plausibly gain in some scenarios by stepping outside the common rule yet the quantitative evidence suggests that remaining in the bloc and pushing for serious joint negotiations with large partners may generate larger and more resilient gains.

Why large members block external negotiations

For Brazil and to a lesser extent Argentina the same counterfactuals look very different. Average welfare changes from bloc negotiations with China and the United States are modest relative to GDP while losses are heavily concentrated in a few manufacturing industries such as apparel textiles leather products and some machinery.

The model quantifies those sectoral losses and shows that workers and firms in import competing manufacturing lose far more than the aggregate numbers suggest. This helps explain why governments that benefit slightly in the aggregate may still face intense lobbying against deep external liberalization and why Mercosur can stay intact while large negotiations repeatedly stall.

Distributional tension inside the bloc

Within Brazil and Argentina export oriented agriculture and resource sectors often gain from lower partner tariffs while urban manufacturing clusters lose. The paper uses industry level data to show how those conflicting interests map into measurable changes in output profits and employment by sector.

Takeaway for customs union stability

Putting all scenarios together the paper argues that customs unions can be internally stable even when they are external negotiation stumbling blocks. Small members gain a lot from joint deals and have little incentive to leave while large members tolerate the union but use their veto power to protect politically sensitive sectors from rapid liberalization.
